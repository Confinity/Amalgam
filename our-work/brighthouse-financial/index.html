Where the system was breaking down

Brighthouse Financial ran ~100 daily batch jobs across datasets with tables nearing 500 million rows, and volume growth was making runs increasingly unstable.

The work had to strengthen the weak loops in the operating system before more complexity piled on top of them.

Brighthouse Financial is a leading U.S.-based life insurance and annuity provider managing more than $240 billion in assets. As data volumes increased across daily operational workloads, they needed stronger batch reliability to protect reporting timelines and internal confidence in system outputs.

Core constraints we had to address

  • Date-based queries on very large tables could take nearly 30 minutes due to missing indexes
  • Recurring SSIS batch failures increased as upstream files grew in size and memory pressure
  • Frequent manual interventions stretched limited internal resources and increased SLA risk
  • Business users uploaded reports without timely data availability, reducing trust in the system
  • System scope had evolved with limited documentation, making troubleshooting slower and riskier

How we stabilized the system

We joined midstream in a production support and development role with one priority: stabilize daily operations first. We investigated failures, performed deep root-cause analysis, fixed recurring issues permanently, optimized database and SSIS bottlenecks, and documented system dependencies so the team could move out of firefighting mode.

The sequence focused on the parts of the workflow where reliability, usability, and system clarity were under the most pressure.

What was the core constraint in the Brighthouse Financial engagement?+

Brighthouse Financial ran ~100 daily batch jobs across datasets with tables nearing 500 million rows, and volume growth was making runs increasingly unstable. One early constraint was: Date-based queries on very large tables could take nearly 30 minutes due to missing indexes.

What moved forward after implementation?+

Critical query runtimes dropped from nearly 30 minutes to seconds, recurring failures were eliminated, and batch operations became reliably scalable. A concrete shift was: Critical query and batch-processing bottlenecks were reduced from nearly half an hour to near-real-time response windows.

Which implementation choices mattered most in this engagement?+

The work centered on SQL Server, SSIS, and Database Indexing. The choices mattered because they supported the real operating constraint instead of adding unnecessary complexity.

How do you keep this kind of work practical instead of over-engineered?+

By sequencing around the highest-leverage constraint, using technology choices that fit the operating context, and staying focused on what actually needs to move for the team.

What is the first move when systems are running but unstable?+

The first move is usually a focused systems read: identify where reliability is breaking down, then address the highest-risk loop first.

How do you modernize without creating more operational risk?+

The approach is phased and practical. This engagement started with "Production-First Stabilization" and expanded from there once core stability improved.

What matters most when complexity increases during growth?+

Clear ownership, reliable operating loops, and stronger coordination patterns become critical so growth does not erode delivery confidence.

Prioritized every live batch incident, diagnosed root causes quickly, and coordinated fixes across upstream/downstream owners to protect daily run continuity.
