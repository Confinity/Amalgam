Why AI agents break in real-world work

This article translates Anthropic's Project Vend findings into practical rollout logic, so teams can avoid predictable agent failures in live operations.

8 min read

Based on research from: Anthropic - Project Vend: Phase two

In 60 seconds

  • Better models improved performance, but did not remove real-world failure modes.
  • Agent rollout fails when teams skip authority, review, escalation, and recovery design.
  • If you are planning autonomy now, verify control readiness before expanding scope.

Who should act: Leaders and operators deciding whether to launch or expand autonomous agents this quarter.

Opening problem

Agent demos create a dangerous illusion: if the model can complete a task once, the workflow is ready for autonomy. In live operations, that assumption breaks fast.

Teams tend to underestimate edge cases, social dynamics, and adversarial behavior. They overestimate how far raw model capability will carry them without operating design.

The pattern in Project Vend is clear: better tools improved performance, but structural fragility still showed up the moment reality got messy.

What the research shows

Scaffolding helped, but did not solve reliability

Phase two improved the setup with stronger models, better tooling, and manager-style oversight. Business performance improved and negative-margin weeks were reduced.

That progress is important. It shows architecture and tool design matter. But it did not remove failure modes tied to social pressure, manipulation, and context ambiguity.

Good-faith performance does not equal adversarial readiness

Anthropic reports that the system handled many good-faith interactions better, yet remained vulnerable with adversarial users.

That gap is exactly what teams miss in rollout planning: the world is not a benchmark. It includes incentives to exploit weaknesses.

Human support remained essential

Even after improvements, the agents still needed substantial human support, both for real-world execution and for recovering from difficult customer situations.

The practical lesson is not that agents are useless. It is that autonomy without recovery design is brittle.

  • Escalation paths are not optional.
  • Guardrails must be tuned to real behavior, not ideal behavior.
  • External red teaming surfaces risks internal teams normalize over time.

What this means in practice

Roll out by authority layer, not by excitement level

Start with low-consequence tasks where errors are recoverable. Keep high-consequence decisions under explicit human review until you have stable evidence.

This staged authority model creates learning without exposing the business to avoidable downside.

Design the review system before turning agents on

A recurring failure pattern is shipping the agent first and inventing oversight later. That reverses the correct order.

Define ownership, exception handling, and kill-switch conditions before launch so the team knows exactly how to intervene.

Treat adversarial pressure as a default condition

If a workflow has economic or social stakes, assume someone will stress it. Build testing and monitoring around that reality.

The practical takeaway here is simple: operational resilience is the product.

Before rollout, verify these 3 controls

  • Authority boundaries are explicit: what is autonomous, what requires human approval.
  • Review and escalation owners are named with response expectations.
  • Rollback conditions are documented and tested before launch.

Do not deploy into these conditions

  • Deploying agents into unstable workflows that are still being redesigned weekly.
  • Launching with unclear ownership for incident handling and quality review.
  • Scaling autonomy without a review layer, escalation logic, or rollback trigger.

What to do this week

Decision

Choose one constrained workflow for autonomy and define the authority boundary now.

Owner

One rollout owner plus one incident-response owner.

Deadline

Within 7 days.

Success signal

A signed-off launch plan exists with authority limits, escalation path, and rollback condition.

Key takeaways

  • Model quality helps, but rollout safety is determined by operating controls.
  • Do not launch autonomy until authority, escalation, and rollback logic are explicit.
  • Scale only after reliability evidence holds in real operating conditions.
